#### Problem 1

!!! example "Problem 1"
    Convert $0.045\,\unit{kg}$ to $\unit{g}$.


??? success "Answer"
    $$ \conv{0.045 \cn{\unit{kg}}}{1} \times \conv{1000 \unit{g}}{1 \cn{\unit{kg}}} = 45 \unit{g}$$



??? tip "Walkthrough"
    Within the metric system, $1 \unit{kg} = 1000 \unit{g}$. Set up the conversion factor with **kg** in the denominator so it cancels with the starting value. 


    $$ \overfactor{\conv{0.045 \cn{\unit{kg}}}{1}}{Starting Quantity} \times \factor{\conv{1000 \unit{g}}{1 \cn{\unit{kg}}}}{g to kg factor} = 45 \unit{g} $$




    Note: The factors can be written in any order. Track the units to determine the arrangement.


    **Quick mental check:** *kilo* means ×1000, so converting from **kg** to **g** shifts the decimal *three* places to the right. $0.045 \to 45$.

!!! example "Problem 3"
    Convert 750 mcg to mg.

??? question "Hint — Metric prefix ladder"
    | Prefix | Symbol | Relative to base unit |
    |---|---|---|
    | **kilo** | k | × 1,000 |
    | *(base)* | g, L, m | × 1 |
    | **milli** | m | ÷ 1,000 |
    | **micro** | mc *(nursing)*, μ | ÷ 1,000,000 |

    To convert: count the steps between prefixes, then multiply or divide by the appropriate factor of 1,000.

??? success "Answer"
    $$\conv{750 \cn{\mcg}}{1} \times \conv{1 \mg}{1000 \cn{\mcg}} = 0.75 \mg$$

??? tip "Walkthrough"
    The conversion factor is $1\,\mg = 1000\,\mcg$. Set it up with $\mcg$ in the denominator so it cancels with the starting value.


    **Watch the direction:** mcg is a *smaller* unit than mg, so converting from mcg to mg gives a *smaller number*. he answer (0.75) is less than 1. No medication is being removed; each milligram simply contains 1000 micrograms, so 750 of them is a fraction of one mg.


    **Quick mental check:** 1000 mcg would be exactly 1 mg. 750 mcg is three-quarters of that — 0.75 mg.

!!! example "Problem 1"
    A patient is ordered 1000 mL of fluid over 8 hours. The IV set delivers
    15 gtt/mL. What is the drip rate in gtt/min?


??? question "Hint — Drip rate setup"
    Drip rate (gtt/min) chains three pieces: the **ordered rate** (mL per hr),
    an **hr → min** conversion, and the **drip factor** (gtt per mL).
    Arrange them so mL and hr both cancel, leaving gtt/min.


??? success "Answer"
    $$\conv{1000\,\cn{\mL}}{8\,\cn{\unit{hr}}} \times \conv{1\,\cn{\unit{hr}}}{60\,\unit{min}} \times \conv{15\,\unit{gtt}}{1\,\cn{\mL}} = \frac{15000\,\unit{gtt}}{480\,\unit{min}} \approx 31\,\unit{gtt/min}$$




??? tip "Walkthrough"
    Three factors, labeled:

    $$\overfactor{\conv{1000\,\mL}{8\,\unit{hr}}}{ordered rate} \times \factor{\conv{1\,\unit{hr}}{60\,\unit{min}}}{hr to min} \times \overfactor{\conv{15\,\unit{gtt}}{1\,\mL}}{drip factor}$$

    The mL cancels between the first and third factors; the hr cancels between the first and second. What remains is gtt/min — exactly the unit asked for. The factors can be written in any order — the units drive the arrangement.

    **Quick mental check:** 1000 mL over 8 hr is 125 mL/hr. About 2 mL/min. At 15 drops per mL, that's roughly 31 drops/min.

!!! example "Problem 4"
    A patient weighs 143 lb. Convert their weight to kg.

??? question "Hint — Weight conversion"
    In the US, patient weight is recorded in **pounds (lb)**, but medication dosing is calculated using **kilograms (kg)**. This conversion is common.

    Conversion factor: $1\,\kg = 2.2\,\unit{lb}$

??? success "Answer"
    $$\conv{143 \cn{\unit{lb}}}{1} \times \conv{1 \kg}{2.2 \cn{\unit{lb}}} = 65 \kg$$

??? tip "Walkthrough"
    $$\overfactor{\conv{143 \unit{lb}}{1}}{patient weight} \times \factor{\conv{1 \kg}{2.2 \unit{lb}}}{conversion factor} = 65 \kg$$

    The conversion factor $1\,\kg = 2.2\,\unit{lb}$ is arranged with $\unit{lb}$ in the denominator so it cancels with the patient's weight, leaving kg.

    **Quick mental check:** a kilogram is a bit more than two pounds, so the kg number should be a little under *half* the pound number. Half of 143 is about 72 — 65 sits just below that. If you got ~315, you multiplied instead of divided.

## Section 4 — Weight-Based Dosing (Adult)

!!! example "Problem 10"
    **Order:** gentamicin 5 mg/kg IV &nbsp;&nbsp;
    **Patient weight:** 80 kg &nbsp;&nbsp;
    **Stock:** 40 mg/mL&nbsp;&nbsp;

    **How many mL?**

??? success "Answer"
    \[\frac{80 \cancel{\text{ kg}}}{1} \times \frac{5 \cancel{\text{ mg}}}{1 \cancel{\text{ kg}}} \times \frac{1 \text{ mL}}{40 \cancel{\text{ mg}}} = 10 \text{ mL}\]

!!! example "Problem 11"
    **Order:** vancomycin 15 mg/kg IV&nbsp;&nbsp;
    **Patient** weight: 176 lb&nbsp;&nbsp;
    **Stock:** 500 mg/10 mL 

    **How many mL?**

??? success "Answer"

Convert weight:

$$\dfrac{176 \cancel{\text{ lb}}}{1}\times \frac{1 \text{ kg}}{2.2 \cancel{\text{ lb}}} = 80 \text{ kg}$$
    
Full chain:

$$\frac{80 \cancel{\text{ kg}}}{1} \times \frac{15 \cancel{\text{ mg}}}{1 \cancel{\text{ kg}}} \times \frac{10 \text{ mL}}{500 \cancel{\text{ mg}}} = 24 \text{ mL}$$


!!! example "Problem 12"
    **Order:** ibuprofen 10 mg/kg orally, max 600 mg &nbsp;&nbsp;
    **Patient** weight: 85 kg &nbsp;&nbsp;
    **Stock:** 200 mg/5 mL

    **How many mL?**

??? success "Answer"

Convert weight:


$$\dfrac{85 \cancel{\text{ kg}}}{1} \times \frac{10 \text{ mg}}{1 \cancel{\text{ kg}}} = 850 \text{ mg}$$
    
850 mg exceeds max of 600 mg. **Use 600 mg** to find volume.
    
Find volume:

$$\frac{600 \cancel{\text{ mg}}}{1} \times \frac{5 \text{ mL}}{200 \cancel{\text{ mg}}} = 15 \text{ mL}$$

## Section 6 — IV Flow Rates

!!! example "Problem 17"
    Order: 1000 mL 0.9% NaCl over 8 hours
    What is the flow rate in mL/hr?



??? success "Answer"
    \[\frac{1000 \text{ mL}}{8 \text{ hr}} = 125 \text{ mL/hr}\]



    \[\dfrac{1000 \text{ mL}}{8 \text{ hr}} = 125 \text{ mL/hr}\]




    \[\dfrac{1000 \text{ mL}}{8 \text{ hr}} = 125 \text{ mL/hr}\]



!!! example "Problem 18"
    Order: 250 mL over 45 minutes
    What is the flow rate in mL/hr?


??? success "Answer"
    Convert time:
    \[45 \cancel{\text{ min}} \times \frac{1 \text{ hr}}{60 \cancel{\text{ min}}} = 0.75 \text{ hr}\]
    
    Rate:
    \[\frac{250 \text{ mL}}{0.75 \text{ hr}} = 333.3 \text{ mL/hr}\]
    
    Round to: **333 mL/hr**


!!! example "Problem 19"
    A 1000 mL bag is running at 150 mL/hr.

    After 3 hours how much fluid remains and how much longer will it run?



??? success "Answer"

Volume infused:

$$\dfrac{150 \text{ mL}}{1 \cancel{\text{hr}}} \times \dfrac{3 \cancel{\text{ hr}}}{1} = 450 \text{ mL}$$
    

Volume remaining:
$$ 1000 \text{ mL} - 450\text{ mL} = 550 \text{ mL} $$


Time remaining:

$$\dfrac{550 \text{ mL}}{1} \times \dfrac {1 \text{ hr}}{{150 \text{ mL}}} = 3.67 \text{ hr}$$
    

Convert partial hour to minutes: 

Total time = 3 hours + 0.67 hours

$$\dfrac{0.67 \text{ hr}}{1} \times \dfrac{1 \text{ hr}}{60 \text{ min}} = 40 \text{ min}$$
    
**3 hours and 40 minutes remaining**

!!! example "Problem 23"

    Stock: 25,000 units in 500 mL 0.9% NaCl

    \[\frac{25{,}000 \text{ units}}{500 \text{ mL}} = 50 \text{ units/mL}\]

!!! example "Problem 24"
    Order: dopamine 5 mcg/kg/min IV
    Patient weight: 60 kg
    Stock: dopamine 400 mg in 250 mL D5W
    What is the flow rate in mL/hr?




??? success "Answer"
Dose per minute:



$$ \dfrac{60 \cancel{\text{ kg}}}{1} \times \frac{5 \text{ mcg}}{1 \cancel{\text{ kg}} \cdot \text{min}} = 300 \text{ mcg/min}$$
    



Convert to mg/hr:


$$\dfrac{300 \cancel{\text{ mcg}}{1 \text{min}} \times \frac{1 \cancel{\text{ mg}}}{1000 \cancel{\text{ mcg}}} \times \frac{60 \text{ min}}{1 \text{ hr}} = 18 \text{ mg/hr}$$
    


Concentration:
    \[\frac{400 \text{ mg}}{250 \text{ mL}} = 1.6 \text{ mg/mL}\]
    
    Flow rate:
    \[\frac{18 \text{ mg/hr}}{1.6 \text{ mg/mL}} = 11.25 \text{ mL/hr}\]
    
    Round to: **11 mL/hr**



!!! example "Problem 25"
